The Impact of Rising Interest Rates on Growth Stocks

Sarah Jenkins
Senior Market Analyst
Oct 24, 2023
8 min read

When central banks announce rate hikes, the immediate reaction in the stock market is often a sharp sell-off, particularly in the technology and growth sectors. But to be a research-driven investor, we must understand why this happens mathematically, rather than just following the panic selling of the crowd.

The Discount Rate Mechanism

The fundamental value of any company is the present value of its future cash flows. To calculate "present value," analysts use a discount rate. This rate is heavily influenced by the "risk-free rate" (typically the yield on a 10-year Treasury bond).

When interest rates rise, the discount rate rises. Let's look at what this means for two different types of companies:

  • Value Companies: Generate significant cash flow today. A higher discount rate affects them, but because their cash flows are near-term, the mathematical penalty is lower.
  • Growth Companies: Promise massive cash flows 5, 10, or 15 years in the future. Because those cash flows are further out, a higher discount rate heavily penalizes their present value calculation.

Research Before Reacting

Hype-driven investors sell immediately on rate hike news. Research-driven investors look deeper. If a growth stock drops 30% because of a 50 basis point rate hike, the market may have overreacted to the discount rate adjustment while ignoring the company's actual fundamental execution.

"Don't invest blindly based on macro headlines. Use research, education, and advisor-backed insight to determine if a valuation drop is a warning sign or an opportunity."

Conclusion for Investors

Understanding the mechanics of valuation allows you to invest with clarity. The next time rates rise, check the underlying thesis of your watched assets on Rivava's Research Hub. If the long-term cash flow generation remains intact, a lower valuation might just be a better entry point.
