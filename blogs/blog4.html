Diversification Strategies: Are You Actually Diversified?

Chittresh, CMO
Oct 18, 2023 8 min read

The concept of diversification is often summarized as "don't put all your eggs in one basket." While true, this oversimplification leads many investors to a false sense of security. If you hold 15 different technology stocks, you are not diversified; you just own 15 baskets in the exact same truck.

The Illusion of Diversification

True diversification is the practice of combining assets that do not move in tandem—assets with low or negative correlation. If Asset A drops due to an economic shock, Asset B should ideally remain stable or increase in value. This smooths out the volatility of the overall portfolio without necessarily sacrificing returns.

"Diversification is the only free lunch in investing." - Harry Markowitz

Many modern investors suffer from "di-worsification"—adding more and more mediocre assets to a portfolio simply for the sake of variety, which dilutes the impact of their highest-conviction ideas while still failing to protect against systemic shocks.

Building Uncorrelated Portfolios

1. Asset Class Diversification

The foundation of diversification lies in asset classes: Equities (Stocks), Fixed Income (Bonds), Real Estate, Commodities, and Cash. Historically, bonds have provided a ballast against equity market downturns, though recent macroeconomic conditions (like simultaneous rising interest rates and inflation) have challenged the traditional 60/40 portfolio model.

2. Geographic Diversification

Home country bias is a significant risk. If your income, your real estate, and 100% of your equity portfolio are tied to a single country's economy, you are highly exposed to localized regulatory changes, currency devaluation, and geopolitical events. Allocating capital to emerging markets and developed international markets provides a necessary hedge.

3. Sector and Factor Diversification

Within equities, diversification means spreading capital across sectors (e.g., Healthcare, Energy, Financials, Technology) and factors (Value vs. Growth, Large Cap vs. Small Cap). A portfolio over-indexed on high-growth tech stocks will suffer disproportionately during interest rate hikes.
